Greg Yates, Principal

I'm Greg Yates. I help small law firms — under fifty people, lawyers included — use AI for the things that actually matter to a law practice: reclaiming time, growing revenue, and making clients glad they hired you.

I started Actionable AI because most of what lawyers were hearing about AI was wrong in one of two ways. It was either breathless ("AI will replace lawyers by 2027!") or paranoid ("never let it near a matter"). Both positions miss the thing that small firms actually need, which is quieter: what should I do on Monday morning?

That's the question this practice answers. Not in the abstract. In your firm. With your stack. This week.

What I bring

Years of work at the intersection of legal operations, software, and the kind of hands-on implementation that only happens when someone sits with your team and actually ships something. I've seen what works in a four-lawyer shop in Tulsa and what fails at a forty-lawyer regional firm. They have more in common than you'd think.

I write a weekly newsletter on Beehiiv about what's actually happening in small-firm AI. I speak at bar associations, law-firm management conferences, and the occasional CLE. I answer my own email.

What I don't do

  • Resell software. If I recommend a tool, it's because it's the right one.
  • Take referral fees or affiliate commissions from vendors.
  • Pretend AI is the answer to everything. It isn't.
  • Work with more firms than I can serve well. That's typically eight to twelve active engagements at a time.

Where I stand on a few things

On "AI replacing lawyers." It won't. What it will do — what it's already doing — is compress the work around lawyers, so the ones who use it well get paid more per hour and the ones who don't slowly become commodities. Small firms have a short window to pick which side of that line they're on.

On privacy and privilege. These are not optional. Every workflow I design accounts for them. If you tell me a client's matter on the phone, I won't put it into a public model. Neither should you.

On vendor hype. If a tool's pricing requires a demo to understand, something is wrong with the tool. If it promises to "transform your practice," it probably won't. If it has a "built specifically for legal" sticker on a generic AI wrapper, it's marking up something you can do yourself for a tenth of the price.

Outside the work

Based in the United States. Reader. Writer. I take a lot of walks. I think the best business advice I've ever heard is some variation of "do the boring thing carefully for a long time." That's what this practice is. The boring thing, carefully, for a long time.
