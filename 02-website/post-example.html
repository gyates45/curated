Workflow

The one workflow every small firm should automate first.

2026·04·22 · 6 min read · by Greg Yates

If you only automate one workflow this year, make it intake-to-engagement. It's where most firms leak time, revenue, and client goodwill — and it's the easiest place to get a fast, obvious win.

Here's the version that works in any firm with Microsoft 365, Clio (or a similar practice management tool), and one AI chat tool. You don't need to buy anything else.

The problem

Most small firms still handle intake like this. The phone rings. Someone takes notes. The notes sit in a notebook or a shared inbox. Eventually — sometimes three days later — a lawyer reviews the notes, drafts a conflict check, pulls an engagement letter, edits it, sends it, waits for a signature, and opens the matter.

That's five to seven separate handoffs. Each handoff loses a little fidelity. Each delay costs a little goodwill. And every step is something that could happen in under an hour if the right pieces were connected.

Most firms don't lose deals to better lawyers. They lose deals to faster lawyers.

The four-step version

1. Capture the intake on one form

One web form. Microsoft Forms or Google Forms works. The form collects: prospective client name, contact info, nature of the matter in their own words, urgency, referral source, and any conflict-check essentials. Embed it on your site. Share the link in signatures. Done.

2. Summarize with AI

When a submission lands, the form contents go into a prompt template you've saved in Claude or ChatGPT. The prompt asks the model to produce three things: a one-paragraph plain-English summary of the matter, a list of potential conflicts to check, and a proposed next step.

You read the output. You either accept, edit, or reject. Ninety percent of the time you accept with one small edit.

3. Pre-draft the engagement letter

A second prompt takes the same intake form and pre-fills your standard engagement letter template with the client name, matter description, and scope. It doesn't send it. It doesn't file it. It stages a draft in your documents folder, ready for review.

Partner reviews in five minutes instead of forty-five. Signs off. Sends.

4. Open the matter

Final step is still manual — and should be. When the engagement letter comes back signed, someone at the firm opens the matter in Clio with the now-clean metadata. This takes two minutes instead of twenty because everything upstream was already clean.

What this gets you

Firms I've run this workflow with typically see intake-to-engagement-letter turnaround drop from three days to four hours. Some faster. One firm got it under ninety minutes because their partner had learned to trust the AI draft enough to sign it on his phone.

The time saved is real. But it's not the biggest thing. The biggest thing is that clients notice. A four-hour turnaround versus three days is the difference between "this firm is on it" and "this firm is probably too busy for me." Referrals track from there.

What this doesn't get you

This isn't a silver bullet. The AI summaries are not legal advice. The engagement letters need a partner's eye. Conflicts still need to be checked against your actual database — the AI can flag names, not run the check.

If you run this workflow badly, you'll create more work, not less. If you run it well, you'll wonder why you ever did it any other way.

Where to start

This week: build the form. It takes twenty minutes. Even if you never add the AI piece, a single intake form already beats the phone-note-inbox status quo by a wide margin.

Next week: write the summarization prompt. Test it against three of your last intake notes. Adjust until it sounds like you.

The week after: write the engagement-letter draft prompt. Same process.

By the end of the month, you have the workflow. By the end of the quarter, you wonder what you were doing before.
